Note that route choice (Monitor Ridge vs. Worm Flows) can depend on snowmelt and road access to Climbers Bivouac, especially earlier in June.</p>

            <h2>The Challenge: Mountain & Routes</h2>

            <p>Understanding the mountain's history, the physical demands, and the specifics of the potential routes is foundational. The 1980 eruption dramatically reshaped the landscape. Always verify current road access and route conditions before your climb. The climb is generally considered "non-technical" by some experienced climbers in good conditions, meaning there are no vertical cliffs or "crazy steep drop-offs" requiring ropes. However, for June, significant snow cover means snow travel skills and appropriate gear are essential for safety on steep snow slopes.</p>

                <h3>The 1980 Eruption & Its Legacy</h3>

                <p>On May 18, 1980, a massive eruption decapitated Mount St. Helens, reducing its height by over 1,300 feet and creating the distinctive horseshoe-shaped crater. The climbing routes ascend through areas profoundly impacted by this event. You'll traverse ash fields and pumice plains, witnessing the raw power of nature and the remarkable ecological recovery that has occurred since. The area is a living laboratory, a testament to resilience.</p>

                <p><font size="-1">The eruption not only changed the mountain's physical form but also its ecological trajectory, making your climb a journey through a recovering landscape.</font></p>

            </div>


            <div class="card-90s">

                <h3>Route Options for June</h3>

                <p>The primary route choice in June depends on snowmelt and the accessibility of the road to Climbers Bivouac. Always check with the Mount St. Helens National Volcanic Monument or USFS for the latest conditions.</p>

                

                <h4>Monitor Ridge Route (Summer Route)</h4>

                <p><font size="-1">This is the most common route once the road to Climbers Bivouac is open and snow has melted sufficiently on the lower trail.</font></p>

                <ul>

                    <li><strong>Start Point:</strong> Climbers Bivouac (~3,800 ft).</li>

                    <li><strong>Distance:</strong> Approximately 8-10 miles round trip (about 5 miles to summit).</li>

                    <li><strong>Elevation Gain:</strong> Approximately 4,500 - 4,800 feet.</li>

                    <li><strong>Typical Time:</strong> 7-10 hours round trip (4-6 hours up, 2-4 hours down, depending on fitness and glissading).</li>

                    <li><strong>Characteristics:</strong>

                        <ul>

                            <li><strong>Stage 1 (Forested):</strong> Ptarmigan Trail #216A, ~2 miles, gradual ascent, good warm-up.</li>

                            <li><strong>Stage 2 (Above Treeline):</strong> Boulder fields, ash, pumice, marked by wooden posts. Navigation critical.</li>

                            <li><strong>Stage 3 (Monitor Ridge proper):</strong> Broad ridge, predominantly snow-covered in June. Follow boot pack or navigate carefully.</li>

                            <li><strong>Stage 4 (Summit/Crater Rim):</strong> Final push up steeper snow slopes. Be mindful of cornices!</li>

                        </ul>

                    </li>

                    <li><strong>Note:</strong> Key landmarks like Loowit Viewpoint and the "Weather Station" can aid navigation. Pay close attention to marker posts.</li>

                </ul>


                <h4>Worm Flows Route (Winter Route / Early Summer Alternative)</h4>

                <p><font size="-1">Used when Climbers Bivouac is inaccessible due to snow on the road. This route is longer and involves more elevation gain.</font></p>

                <ul>

                    <li><strong>Start Point:</strong> Marble Mountain Sno-Park (~2,700 ft).</li>

                    <li><strong>Distance:</strong> Approximately 12 miles round trip (about 6 miles to summit).</li>

                    <li><strong>Elevation Gain:</strong> Approximately 5,700 feet.</li>

                    <li><strong>Typical Time:</strong> 9-12 hours round trip (5-7 hours up, 3-5 hours down, depending on fitness and glissading).</li>

                    <li><strong>Characteristics:</strong> Follows a different ridge system, often entirely on snow in June. Requires strong navigation skills and potentially more extensive snow travel. Marked by poles, but can be harder to follow in poor visibility.</li>

                </ul>

                 <p><font size="-1">Route times are highly dependent on individual fitness, snow conditions, group size, and breaks taken. Plan conservatively.</font></p>

            <h2>Planning Your Ascent!</h2>

            <p>Successful and safe climbs are built on meticulous planning. This involves securing permits, ensuring you meet the physical demands, and possessing the necessary mountaineering skills for June's snow conditions.</p>


            <table width="100%" border="0" cellspacing="10"><tr><td valign="top" width="50%">

            <div class="card-90s">

                <h3>Step 1: Permits - <blink>MANDATORY!</blink></h3>

                <p>Climbing permits are required from April 1 to October 31.</p>

                <ul>

                    <li><strong>Where:</strong> Recreation.gov (Search "Mount St. Helens Climbing Permits").</li>

                    <li><strong>When:</strong> Released in batches. Check Recreation.gov for exact dates/times. They sell out extremely fast, especially for June weekends.</li>

                    <li><strong>Quota:</strong> 110 climbers per day (May 15 - Oct 31).</li>

                    <li><strong>Cost:</strong> Per-person, per-day fee applies.</li>

                    <li><strong>Action:</strong> Print your permit and carry it. Rangers do check.</li>

                </ul>

                <p><font size="-1">Securing a permit is the very first step. Be prepared to book the moment they become available.</font></p>

            </div>

            </td><td valign="top" width="50%">

            <div class="card-90s">

                <h3>Step 2: Physical Fitness - It's Strenuous!</h3>

                <p>Don't underestimate the challenge. The combination of elevation gain, mileage, altitude, and snow travel demands excellent physical condition. If you're in "OK-ish" shape, you can likely do it, but expect to be sore.</p>

                <h4>Training Focus: Relative Effort</h4>

                <div class="chart-container">

                    <canvas id="fitnessChart"></canvas>

                </div>

                <p><font size="-1">Focus on cardiovascular endurance (hiking with a weighted pack, running, cycling), strength training (legs, core), and practice on steep, uneven terrain (snow if possible). Aim to be comfortable hiking 8-12 miles with significant elevation gain before attempting MSH. Consider training on a stairmaster or similar incline machine, and do some training hikes with a weighted pack (15-20 lbs) to simulate climb conditions. Even a few focused training hikes can help with conditioning, gear familiarity, and mental preparation.</font></p>

            </div>

            </td></tr></table>

                <h3>Step 3: Skills & Experience - Essential for June Snow</h3>

                <p>June climbs involve significant snow travel. The following skills are not just recommended, they are essential for your safety. While some very experienced climbers familiar with the mountain and current conditions might occasionally make different gear choices, for the general climber in June, proficiency with snow travel tools is key for managing steep snow slopes safely.</p>

                

                <div>

                    <div class="accordion-toggle-90s" data-target="skill-snow">🏔️ Snow Travel Skills (Crampons & Ice Axe) ▼</div>

                    <div class="accordion-content-90s" id="skill-snow">

                        <p><font size="-1">Proper use of mountaineering boots and crampons for walking, traversing slopes, and kicking steps. Ice axe skills include self-belay (using the axe for balance and probing snow) and, most crucially, <strong>self-arrest</strong> (stopping a fall on a snow slope). Practice self-arrest thoroughly in a safe environment before your climb. Know how to glissade safely if conditions permit.</font></p>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div>

                    <div class="accordion-toggle-90s" data-target="skill-nav">🧭 Navigation Skills (Map, Compass, GPS) ▼</div>

                    <div class="accordion-content-90s" id="skill-nav">

                        <p><font size="-1">The route above treeline can be obscured by snow, fog, or whiteout conditions, making navigation critical. Proficiency with a topographic map, compass, and altimeter is vital. A GPS device or smartphone with downloaded offline maps (e.g., Gaia GPS, CalTopo) and a fully charged backup battery pack is highly recommended. Crucially, you must know how to use these tools effectively in challenging conditions and how to follow marker posts, both up and down.</font></p>

                    </div>

                </div>

                 <div>

                    <div class="accordion-toggle-90s" data-target="skill-glissade">⚠️ Glissading Know-How (Controlled Descent) ▼</div>

                    <div class="accordion-content-90s" id="skill-glissade">

                        <p><font size="-1">Glissading (controlled sliding down snow slopes) can be a fun and efficient way to descend, but only if done safely. Requires knowing how to use your ice axe as a brake and rudder. <strong>ALWAYS remove crampons before glissading.</strong> Scout your path carefully for rocks, trees, or other hazards, and ensure a safe runout. Use your heels and hiking poles (if you have them) as additional brakes if needed. Follow established 'butt tracks' if available but always verify the safety of the path. If in doubt, walk down.</font></p>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <p style="margin-top:10px;"><font size="-1">If you are new to these skills, strongly consider hiring a certified mountain guide or taking a comprehensive mountaineering skills course. Your safety depends on it.</font></p>

            </div>
